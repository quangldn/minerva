Keir Starmer is out, Andy Burnham is in. On Monday, outgoing Prime Minister Starmer traveled to Buckingham Palace to tender his resignation to King Charles III. Shortly afterward, his successor, Andy Burnham, made the same journey and was invited by the king to form a new government. Both are from the center-left Labour Party.

Burnham then returned to Downing Street, where he delivered his first remarks as prime minister. "I want to be honest with you: we have not been good enough and we need to be better. We will be," he said, adding that his generation of politicians must "raise our game" and that Britain must "regain our stability."

Burnham becomes the U.K.'s seventh prime minister in a decade, since Britons voted in June 2016 to leave the European Union — a decision that shrank the British economy, triggered anger at the governing class, and contributed to a revolving door of prime ministers.

Starmer led Labour to a landslide victory two years ago, but his personal approval ratings sank amid a belief that he failed to deliver change, criticism over the Peter Mandelson affair, and losses to a populist anti-immigration party in May's local elections. He resigned as Labour leader on June 22, and Burnham assumed that role Friday. Under Britain's parliamentary system, the ruling party can replace its leader — and therefore the prime minister — without a general election.

A former MP of 16 years and mayor of Greater Manchester with a working-class image, Burnham promised measures to help with the cost of living, a pledge to end rough sleeping, and a 10-year plan for Britain. European leaders including Ireland's Micheal Martin and Germany's Friedrich Merz congratulated him, while President Trump praised reports Burnham might ease a ban on North Sea oil and gas drilling.
